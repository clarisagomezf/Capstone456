This first code chunk is parsing data from the credits.csv column labeled cast and includes information about primary and secondary characters of the film including gender. Then, I merged with the movies dataset to get budget and revenue.

parse_cast <- function(cast_text) {
  text <- 
    sub("\\\\$\\", "",    #drop the first one
        gsub("\'|\"|\\[|\\]|\\{|\\}|,", "",
             gsub("\'cast_id\':|\'character\':|\'credit_id\':|\'gender\':|\'id\':|\'name\':|\'order\':|\'profile_path\':", 
                  "$", 
                  cast_text)
        )
    )
  
  data <- data.frame(matrix(
    trimws(unlist(
        strsplit(text, "\\\\$\\")
        )), 
    ncol = 8, byrow = TRUE), stringsAsFactors = FALSE)
  
  names(data) <- c("cast_id", "character", "credit_id", "gender", "id", "name", "order", "profile_path")
  data$order <- as.numeric(data$order)
  
  return(data %>% select(cast_id, gender, id, name, order))
}

To answer some questions we have about gender representation in media, I found a dataset that contains information on >45000 movies released in or before July 2017. This information includes the movie title, cast, gender of each cast member (our most ideal variable - 0, 1, or 2 - Unspecified, Female, Male, respectively), movie budget, revenue, genre, and order of importance of each cast member (0-n if n is the number of cast members).

For our project, one thing we have been wanting to investigate is if there are patterns of differences when our protagonist is female compared to when our protagonist is male. After parsing and cleaning my data, I wanted to observe just how much more budget male protagnist movies get than female protagonist movies. Thus, the question my visualization aims to answer is: How much budget does a movie get when the main character is male compared to female? This question is important for showing the inequality and perhaps the inequity in the film industry.

To compare the movie budgets, I graphed the top 10 highest budget movies for each gender in descending order on a column plot, where the darker colored columns are male protagonist movies and the lighter colored columns are female protagonist movies, with the movie titles along the x-axis. I decided to divide budget by one million for visualization purposes.

Interestingly so, the highest budgeted movie with a female protagonist has roughly the same budget as the fifth and sixth lowest-ranked budgeted movies with male protagonists, falling 120 million behind the highest budgeted movie with a male protagonist. The remaining 9 female protagonist movies fall behind all 10 highest male budgeted movies.

Another takeaway is that the highest budgeted movie with a female protagonist Tangled is an animated film, and the voice actor, may not actually be getting as much as Johnny Depp in Pirates of the Caribbean. So that budgeted money may actually be going more towards production, rather than the female actress. In total, there are three animated movies in the top 10 highest budgeted movies with female protagonists, whereas there are none in the top 10 for male protagonists. Therefore it may be that there is more investment in movies that are animated due to production, and those animated films happen to have female protagonists. So my question is do they not want to invest as much if there is a real, live action female protagonist?

It would be nice to have a variable of how much the actors are getting and comparing that as well (voice actors are paid a lot less I believe).
